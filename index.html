Daniel Frey

Dark-Field CT · Machine Learning

PhD candidate at Technical University of Munich (TUM), affiliated with the Chair of Biomedical Physics and the Munich Institute of Biomedical Engineering, in close collaboration with the Chair for AI in Healthcare and Medicine. My research focuses on developing techniques to advance the clinical translation of human-scale dark-field computed tomography, in particular on streak artifact reduction and image quality enhancement using representation learning. Ultimately, my work aims at closing the gap between physics-based domain knowledge and modern machine learning for clinical applications.

I began my academic journey with a Bachelor's degree in Biochemistry at TUM, followed by a second Bachelor's degree in Physics at the same university, where I was first introduced to X-ray dark-field CT. For my Master's degree, I joined the Biomedical Engineering and Medical Physics (BEMP) program at TUM, culminating in a thesis conducted at the human-scale prototype. In parallel to my studies, I collected practical know-how while working at the TÜV SÜD electrical safety department, and deepened my knowledge on data preparation and deep learning flows in neuroimaging as part of the Morphometry Group at TUM University Hospital.

    IEEE ISBI 2026, London, UK · Conference · Abstract · Poster
    Demonstrates that 3D Gaussian splatting enables streak-reduced dark-field CT reconstruction compared to FDK, especially in sparse-view conditions.
  • Streak-reduced Human-scale Dark-field CT with 3D Gaussian Splatting (poster presentation by co-author)
    IEEE ISBI 2026, London, UK · Conference · Abstract · Poster
    Repurposes 3D Gaussian splatting for post-processing dark-field CT, achieving superior streak reduction compared to conventional filters while preserving edge sharpness and high CNR.
  • X-ray Dark-field CT Radiomics for Lung Phantom Assessment (poster presentation by co-author)
    IEEE ISBI 2026, London, UK · Conference · Abstract · Poster
    Proposes a methodology providing improved phantom material characterization based on radiomic texture features in attenuation and dark-field CT to replicate lung tissue structure.
